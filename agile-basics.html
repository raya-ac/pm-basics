Agile Basics

What Agile actually means — beyond the buzzwords and the bad meetings.

The Agile Manifesto

Written in 2001 by 17 software practitioners who were fed up with heavyweight, documentation-heavy processes. The Manifesto is short and deliberately values-based, not prescriptive.

"We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value..."

"That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more."

Key Principles (from the 12)

  • Deliver working software frequently — weeks rather than months.
  • Welcome changing requirements, even late in development.
  • Business people and developers must work together daily.
  • Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and trust they need.
  • Working software is the primary measure of progress.
  • Sustainable pace — Agile processes promote sustainable development indefinitely, not crunch cycles.
  • Simplicity — the art of maximising the amount of work not done — is essential.
  • The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organising teams.
The manifesto doesn't mention sprints, story points, or standups. Those are practices from specific frameworks (Scrum, SAFe) layered on top. Confusing the framework with the philosophy is a core source of Agile dysfunction.

Scrum

Scrum is the most widely-used Agile framework. It structures work into fixed-length iterations called sprints (usually 1–2 weeks), with defined roles, ceremonies, and artifacts.

Roles

RoleResponsibility
Product Owner (PO) Owns the product backlog. Defines and prioritises what the team builds. Represents stakeholder and customer needs. Accountable for product value.
Scrum Master Facilitates Scrum ceremonies. Removes impediments. Coaches the team on Agile practices. Is not a project manager or team lead.
Development Team Cross-functional group (engineers, designers, QA) who deliver the work. Self-organising — they decide how to accomplish sprint goals.

Ceremonies

Sprint Planning

Team selects backlog items for the sprint and breaks them into tasks. Produces a sprint goal — a single sentence describing what the sprint is meant to achieve.

Daily Standup

15-minute sync. What did I do yesterday? What am I doing today? What's blocking me? Designed to surface blockers, not report status to a manager.

Sprint Review

Team demos completed work to stakeholders. Stakeholders give feedback. Backlog is updated based on what was learned. Not a formal sign-off ceremony.

Retrospective

Team reflects on the process: what went well, what didn't, what to change. Outputs are concrete action items, not just discussion. The most undervalued ceremony.

Artifacts

  • Product Backlog: Ordered list of everything the product might need. Owned by the Product Owner. Continuously refined.
  • Sprint Backlog: The subset of the product backlog selected for the current sprint, plus the plan for delivering it.
  • Increment: The sum of all completed backlog items at the end of a sprint. Must be usable and meet the Definition of Done.

Kanban

Kanban is a flow-based system originating from Toyota's manufacturing process. Unlike Scrum, there are no sprints, no fixed iterations, and no prescribed roles. Work flows continuously from to-do → in progress → done.

Core Principles

  • Visualise the workflow: every piece of work is a card on a board (physical or digital). Everyone can see what's in flight.
  • Limit Work in Progress (WIP): each column has a maximum number of concurrent items. When a column is full, the team must finish existing work before pulling more in. This prevents context-switching and uncovers bottlenecks.
  • Focus on flow: the metric that matters is cycle time — how long it takes a work item to go from start to done. Optimise to make that shorter and more consistent.
  • Make policies explicit: everyone agrees on what "in progress" or "done" actually means for your team.

Common Agile Anti-Patterns

Zombie Standups
The team recites status updates to the Scrum Master while staring at a screen. Nobody mentions blockers because it feels like admitting failure. Standups should be a team conversation, not a report-up ritual.
Story Point Obsession
Teams spend 45 minutes arguing whether a ticket is a 3 or a 5. Points become a performance metric — engineers inflate estimates to look safe. Velocity is a planning tool, not a productivity KPI.
Sprint as Deadline
"It's in the sprint, so it must ship Friday." Sprints are a planning cadence, not a commitment to external stakeholders. Treating them as hard deadlines creates pressure that degrades quality and trust.
Ignored Retro Actions
The team identifies the same three problems every sprint and nothing changes. Retros without follow-through teach the team that the process is performative. Always assign owners and review actions from the last retro before generating new ones.
Backlog as Dumping Ground
The backlog grows to 400 items, most of which are 18 months old and irrelevant. An ungroomed backlog creates noise and erodes the team's trust in the planning process. Ruthlessly archive stale items.
Fake Agile ("ScrumFall")
Sprints exist, but the full scope is locked upfront, estimates are used as commitments, and change is treated as failure. This is Waterfall in Agile clothing. The ceremonies create overhead without delivering any of the benefits.

Estimation Approaches

Story Points

Relative complexity using Fibonacci sequence (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13). Abstracts away hours. Team-specific — a 5 at company A means nothing at company B. Helps track velocity over time.

T-Shirt Sizing

XS / S / M / L / XL. Lower precision but faster. Good for backlog grooming and roadmap conversations. Converts to story points later if needed.

#NoEstimates

A movement arguing that estimation effort is waste. Instead: break work into similarly-sized chunks and count throughput. Focus on reducing batch size rather than predicting it.

Estimates are not commitments. An estimate is a probability distribution, not a promise. The further out the work, the wider the distribution. A roadmap estimate from 3 months away that turns out to be wrong is not a failure — it's expected variance.

For PMs: Getting the Most from Agile

How to Run a Good Retrospective

  • Create psychological safety first. If engineers don't feel safe raising real problems, you'll get sanitised feedback. Set the tone by sharing something that didn't go well from your own side.
  • Use a structured format: Start / Stop / Continue or What went well / What could be improved / Action items.
  • Time-box idea generation before discussion to avoid groupthink — write on sticky notes before sharing.
  • Pick 1–3 action items maximum. More than that and nothing gets done. Assign an owner and a due date.
  • Open the next retro by reviewing actions from the last one.

How to Protect Team Velocity

  • Shield the team from interrupt-driven requests mid-sprint. Stakeholders should enter work via the backlog, not via Slack DMs to engineers.
  • Reduce meeting load. Engineers need uninterrupted blocks for deep work. Every hour of meetings is an hour not building.
  • Don't pull engineers into "quick calls" to explain tickets — write clear acceptance criteria instead.
  • Leave capacity buffer (10–20%) for unplanned work, bug fixes, and tech debt. Fully loaded sprints always slip.

How to Say No Using the Backlog

The backlog is a PM's most powerful prioritisation tool — and a diplomatic shield. When a stakeholder asks for something urgent, the answer isn't "no" — it's "show me what comes out to make room for it." This makes trade-offs explicit and puts the decision with the stakeholder who has the most context.

Maintain a visible, groomed, prioritised backlog. When stakeholders can see the full queue, conversations shift from "why isn't my thing done yet" to "help me understand how to prioritise this relative to what's already in flight."
