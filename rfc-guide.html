Writing

RFCs

How to write a request for comment that gets genuine feedback rather than silence, rubber-stamp approval, or a 200-message thread that never reaches a decision.

What RFCs are actually for

An RFC (Request for Comments) is a document proposing a technical decision and inviting feedback before the decision is made. The key word is before. An RFC written after the implementation is already done is a changelog in disguise — it records what happened rather than genuinely opening options.

RFCs are worth writing when: the decision will be hard to reverse, multiple people need to understand and maintain the outcome, or reasonable engineers would disagree on the right approach. They're not worth writing for every ticket or for decisions that only affect one person's work.

The structure that works

1. Summary (2-4 sentences)

What are you proposing, in plain terms? Someone should be able to read this and immediately know whether they need to read the rest.

2. Problem / motivation

What problem does this solve? Why does it need solving now? Be specific. "Our current approach doesn't scale" is not a problem statement. "Our payment processing queue has exceeded capacity three times in the last month, with the most recent incident causing 12 minutes of downtime" is.

3. Proposal

What exactly are you proposing? This is the longest section. Be concrete about what will change, how it will work, and what the migration path looks like.

4. Alternatives considered

This section is what separates a genuine RFC from a decision memo. List the other approaches you considered and explain why you ruled them out. If you can't articulate why the alternatives are worse, your proposal might not be right either.

5. Drawbacks

What are the downsides of your proposal? Every approach has them. Acknowledging them in the RFC builds trust and often surfaces mitigations. An RFC with no drawbacks section signals that the author hasn't thought critically about their own idea.

6. Open questions

What decisions have you explicitly deferred? What feedback are you specifically looking for? This guides reviewers toward where their input will actually be useful.

Template # RFC: [title]
Author: [name] | Status: Draft | Date: YYYY-MM-DD

Sections: Summary — Problem — Proposal — Alternatives — Drawbacks — Open Questions

Getting genuine feedback

An RFC that gets silence in response is usually one of three things: too long to engage with, proposing something people feel awkward disagreeing with publicly, or sent to too wide an audience with no specific ask.

Fix these by: keeping the doc under 1,500 words for most decisions, actively soliciting disagreement ("I'm specifically unsure about X — does anyone see a flaw here?"), and tagging specific people for specific sections rather than pasting a link in a channel and hoping.

Give people a deadline. "Feedback by Thursday" gets more responses than "whenever you get a chance." Acknowledge feedback explicitly — even if you disagree. People stop commenting when they don't know whether their comments are being read.

Closing the RFC

An RFC should end with a clear decision. Not "we'll revisit this" or "more discussion needed" — a decision and the reasons for it. Document the decision in the RFC itself, not just in a Slack thread that will be impossible to find in six months.

Signs of a healthy RFC culture RFCs are short. Feedback is specific and substantive. Alternatives are taken seriously. Decisions are documented. New team members can read past RFCs and understand why things are the way they are.
