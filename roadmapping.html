Roadmapping

How to build roadmaps that communicate strategy, earn stakeholder trust, and survive contact with reality.

🗺 Outcome vs Output Roadmaps

Output roadmap (feature roadmap)

Lists features and delivery dates. Easy to understand but dangerous: commits to solutions before problems are validated. Creates the illusion of certainty. Engineers hate it because scope always changes but the date never moves.

Example row:
Q2 | Dark mode | Mobile team | June 30

Outcome roadmap (strategy roadmap)

States the problem to solve and the metric to move. Teams decide how to solve it. Flexible on solution, committed on outcome. Works better for alignment because "why" doesn't change as often as "what."

Example row:
Q2 | Reduce checkout abandonment by 15% | Growth team
Best practice: Use outcome roadmaps internally. Produce a feature view for external stakeholders (sales, customers) who need specifics — but clearly label it as directional, not a commitment.

🕐 Now / Next / Later Format

The simplest roadmap format. Avoids false date precision while communicating priority clearly.

Now

In progress or starting this sprint/month. High confidence.

Reduce checkout abandonment (Growth)
SSO login support (Platform)
API rate limiting (Infra)

Next

Planned for this quarter. Scope still being refined.

Mobile push notifications (Mobile)
Bulk export feature (Data)
Onboarding redesign (Growth)

Later

On the radar. Not committed. Subject to change.

Marketplace integrations
AI-powered recommendations
Offline mode

🎯 Prioritisation Frameworks

RICE Scoring

Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort

FactorWhat to estimateScale
ReachUsers affected per quarterRaw number (e.g., 5,000)
ImpactEffect per user if they encounter the feature0.25 / 0.5 / 1 / 2 / 3
ConfidenceHow sure are you of Reach + Impact estimates?50% / 80% / 100%
EffortPerson-months to buildRaw number (e.g., 2)

ICE Scoring

Simpler than RICE. Score = Impact × Confidence × Ease (1–10 each). Good for quick gut-check prioritisation in early discovery. Less rigorous but faster to run across many ideas.

MoSCoW

Must have — required for launch
Should have — high value but not blocking
Could have — nice to have
Won't have — explicit no for this cycle

Best used for scoping a specific release, not a full roadmap.

🤝 Stakeholder Alignment

Step 1
Gather inputs before you plan
1:1s with sales, customer success, engineering leads, and executives. What problems are they hearing most? What's blocking revenue? What tech debt is slowing the team? Input first, roadmap second.
Step 2
Share a draft, not a final plan
Send the draft roadmap with explicit "open questions" and "what we left out." This signals you want input, not a rubber stamp. It also surfaces conflicts early.
Step 3
Run a roadmap review meeting
Walk through strategy first, then specifics. Start with "here's the outcome we're optimizing for this quarter" before showing any feature list. Stakeholders argue less about features when they agree on the goal.
Step 4
Document what was rejected and why
Maintain a "what's not on the roadmap" section. "We evaluated bulk export but deprioritized it because checkout abandonment has 5x more revenue impact." This pre-empts the same request coming back next week.

❌ Saying No Gracefully

"Not now" instead of "no"

"That's on our radar for H2 — we're focused on checkout this quarter because it impacts 3x more users." Gives a reason, acknowledges the request, provides a timeline.

Show the trade-off

"We can add that to Q2 if we move mobile push to Q3. Here's the impact of each — which would you prioritize?" Forces stakeholders to own the trade-off instead of just adding to a wish list.

Tie to strategy

"Our North Star this half is activation rate. This request helps retention, which is next half's focus. Let's revisit it in the Q3 planning cycle."

Capture it publicly

Add rejected items to a public backlog or "considering" list. People push less when they know their idea is recorded and will be reviewed — versus feeling ignored.

🚫 Roadmap Anti-Patterns

Anti-patternSymptomFix
The Gantt chart roadmapEvery item has a start date, end date, and dependency arrow. Looks rigorous; becomes wrong within 2 weeks.Use quarters or Now/Next/Later. Dates belong in sprint plans, not roadmaps.
The wish listRoadmap has 60 items, no priorities, no capacity reality check. Everything is "Q2."Apply capacity constraints. If the team can ship 4 meaningful things a quarter, your roadmap has 4 items.
The sales roadmapRoadmap is built around what sales promised customers, not what users need.Separate "committed" from "planned." Sales commits should be rare and explicitly surfaced.
Never updatedRoadmap is 6 months stale. Nobody trusts it.Review and publish updates monthly. Even "no change" is an update worth making.
No "why"Items on roadmap with no context about what problem they solve.Every roadmap item has a one-line problem statement and a success metric.

📅 Quarterly Planning Cadence

Week -4
Input gathering
Customer interviews, sales feedback, NPS analysis, tech debt review with engineering, data review of current quarter OKRs.
Week -3
Prioritisation workshop
PM + engineering lead + design lead. RICE score top candidates. Agree on top 3-5 bets for the quarter. Identify dependencies and risks.
Week -2
Draft roadmap review
Share with extended stakeholders. Collect feedback. Resolve conflicts. Update "not this quarter" list with explanations.
Week -1
Finalize + publish
Lock the roadmap. Communicate to engineering so sprint planning can start. Share with company all-hands or product newsletter.
Monthly
Checkpoint updates
Review progress, surface blockers, adjust if a bet isn't working. Roadmaps are living documents — monthly updates signal health, not failure.
