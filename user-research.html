User Research

Methods, templates, and synthesis techniques for understanding what users actually need — not just what they say they want.

🔍 Generative vs Evaluative Research

Generative (discovery)

Explores a problem space you don't fully understand yet. Answers "what should we build?" and "what problems exist?"

  • User interviews
  • Contextual inquiry (observe users in their environment)
  • Diary studies
  • Jobs-to-be-Done interviews
Early stageQualitative

Evaluative (validation)

Tests a specific solution or hypothesis. Answers "does this work?" and "is our assumption correct?"

  • Usability testing (can users complete the task?)
  • A/B tests (which variant performs better?)
  • Surveys (is our hypothesis widespread?)
  • First-click tests
Late stageQuant or qual

💬 Interview Guide Template

Golden rule: Ask about behavior, not opinions. "Walk me through the last time you..." beats "What features would you like?" People are bad at predicting what they want; they're great at describing what they've done. 
## Interview Guide — Checkout Flow Discovery
Duration: 45 min | Interviewer: [name] | Date: [date]

### Intro (5 min)
- "This is not a test of you — we're testing our product."
- "There are no wrong answers. Thinking out loud helps us most."
- "May I record this session? It stays internal."

### Warm-up (5 min)
- Tell me a bit about your role and what you use [product] for.
- How long have you been a customer?

### Core questions (25 min)
1. Walk me through the last time you made a purchase on [product].
   - What triggered you to start that session?
   - What did you do first?

2. Was there a moment where you hesitated or had a question?
   - What were you uncertain about?
   - How did you resolve it?

3. Have you ever started a checkout and not completed it?
   - What happened? What did you do instead?

4. What would have made that experience easier?
   [Follow-up: "Tell me more" / "Why was that important to you?"]

### Wrap-up (10 min)
- Is there anything about checkout that we haven't covered?
- If you could change one thing about this process, what would it be?
- Who else do you know who has a different experience with this?

📌 Synthesis Techniques

Affinity Mapping

Write every observation, quote, and behavior on a sticky note (one per note). Group related notes into clusters. Name the clusters. Patterns that emerge are your themes — not pre-set categories but emergent ones.

Tools: FigJam, Miro, physical sticky notes. Do it with the team for shared understanding.

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

Frame user needs as jobs: "When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

Example: "When I'm comparing plans before buying, I want to see total annual cost, so I can justify the purchase to my manager."

JTBD reframes your product from features to outcomes. Useful for roadmap prioritization.

Opportunity-Solution Tree

Link your desired outcome → user opportunities (unmet needs) → solutions (features). Forces you to map solutions back to real user problems before committing to build them.

Insight + Evidence format

Structure findings as: Insight (the pattern) + Evidence (the quotes/data that support it) + Implication (what we should do about it). Separates observation from interpretation.

⚖ Qual vs Quant — When to Use What

Question typeUse qualUse quant
Why do users drop off at step 3?✓ Interviews reveal the reasonFunnel data shows that they drop off — not why
How many users have this problem?Interviews confirm the problem exists✓ Survey or analytics gives the count
Which variant converts better?Usability test explains why one works✓ A/B test measures which one wins
What do users actually do in the app?✓ Observation catches unexpected behaviorAnalytics shows aggregate patterns
Is our new onboarding clearer?✓ Usability test shows where confusion occursCompletion rate shows if it improved overall
Combine both: Qual tells you the story; quant tells you the scale. "Users are confused by the pricing page" (qual) + "42% of trial users view pricing 3+ times before converting" (quant) = a compelling case for a redesign.

🧠 Common Research Biases

Confirmation bias

Designing interview questions that lead to the answer you want. Fix: Have someone who disagrees with your hypothesis review the guide. Ask open-ended questions, not leading ones.

Social desirability bias

Participants say what they think you want to hear. Fix: Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical intent. "Would you use this?" ≠ "Tell me about the last time you tried to do this."

Availability bias

Only interviewing the most vocal or accessible users (power users, Twitter complainers). Fix: Recruit across the full user spectrum, including quiet mid-tier users who represent the majority.

Recency bias

Weighting the most recent interview most heavily. Fix: Synthesize after all interviews are complete, not after each one. Let patterns emerge from the full data set.

📢 Sharing Findings with Engineering

Format
One-page research readout
Context (why we did this research) → Key findings (3–5 insights, each with supporting quotes) → Implications (what we should consider doing) → Open questions (what we still don't know). Engineers read the implications section; skip the methodology.
Quotes
Use verbatim quotes, not paraphrases
"I always forget my password here, so I just use Google login" hits differently than "users prefer social login." Direct quotes build empathy faster than summaries.
Involve engineers early
Bring engineers to research sessions
Even one session changes everything. Hearing a user struggle in real time is worth a dozen research reports. Engineers who've observed users write better code for them.
Repository
Maintain a research repository
Store all research in one searchable place (Dovetail, Notion, Confluence). Tag by theme, persona, product area. Research compounds — a 2-year-old insight is often still valid.
