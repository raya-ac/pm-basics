Prioritisation

Frameworks and mental models for deciding what to build next — and how to defend those decisions under pressure.

🆔 RICE Scoring

Formula: RICE Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort
FactorDefinitionHow to estimateExample
ReachHow many users will be affected per quarter?Use analytics: DAU who touch this flow4,000 users/quarter
ImpactHow much does it move the needle per user?0.25=minimal, 0.5=low, 1=medium, 2=high, 3=massive2 (high)
ConfidenceHow confident are you in Reach and Impact?100%=high, 80%=medium, 50%=low80%
EffortPerson-months to build, design, and QAEng + design estimate in planning2 person-months

Example calculation

Checkout autocomplete feature:
Reach: 4,000 | Impact: 2 | Confidence: 0.8 | Effort: 2
RICE = (4,000 × 2 × 0.8) ÷ 2 = 3,200

Compare against another item:
Dark mode — Reach: 8,000 | Impact: 0.5 | Confidence: 0.8 | Effort: 3
RICE = (8,000 × 0.5 × 0.8) ÷ 3 = 1,067

Despite higher reach, dark mode scores lower because impact-per-user is low and effort is high.

🥶 ICE Scoring

What it is

Score = Impact × Confidence × Ease (each 1–10). Simpler and faster than RICE. Best for early-stage ideas, growth experiments, or any time you need a quick gut-check ranking across 20+ ideas.

When to use ICE vs RICE

ICE: Many small experiments, fast ranking needed, early discovery.
RICE: Larger features, quarterly planning, when you need to defend decisions to stakeholders who'll question your estimates.

ICE Example — Growth experiment backlog:

Idea                          Impact  Confidence  Ease   Score
Add social proof to checkout   8       7           9      504
Simplify signup form           7       9           8      504
Add live chat on pricing page  6       6           7      252
Refactor onboarding email      5       8           9      360

Priority: Checkout social proof = Simplified signup (tie) > Onboarding email > Live chat

🆕 MoSCoW Method

Must Have

Non-negotiable. Without this, the release fails or is not viable. These are legal requirements, core user workflows, or contractual commitments. If in doubt, it's not a Must.

Should Have

High value and expected by users, but the release works without it. You'll build it if you have capacity. It's not a Should if missing it would cause users to reject the product.

Could Have

Nice-to-have. Small delta in value if included. First to be cut when scope needs trimming. These often become the next sprint's Should Haves.

Won't Have (this time)

Explicitly out of scope for this release. Critical for alignment: naming what you're NOT building prevents scope creep. "Won't have" doesn't mean "never" — just "not now."

Watch out: MoSCoW is abused when stakeholders classify everything as "Must Have." Enforce a ratio: Musts should be no more than 60% of total scope. If everything is a Must, nothing is.

📈 Kano Model

Maps features to user satisfaction — not all features are equal, even at the same RICE score.

Basic (Must-be)
Expected. Absence causes dissatisfaction; presence is neutral. Password reset, receipt emails. You lose without them but don't win with them.
Performance (Linear)
More = more satisfied. Page load speed, storage space, number of integrations. RICE scoring works well here.
Delighter (Attractive)
Unexpected; creates delight. Absence doesn't disappoint. Confetti on first purchase, Spotify Wrapped. Drives word-of-mouth.
Indifferent
Users don't care either way. Most internal admin features. Stop building these — they use capacity without moving metrics.

🤏 Handling HiPPO Opinions

HiPPO = Highest Paid Person's Opinion. When a VP or CEO overrides prioritisation with gut feel, it derails roadmaps and demoralizes teams. Here's how to handle it.

Understand the motivation

Most HiPPO requests stem from a real concern: a customer call, a competitor move, a gut feeling about market direction. Ask "what problem are you trying to solve?" before pushing back. The solution they propose may be wrong, but the underlying concern might be valid.

Show the trade-off explicitly

"We can add that to Q2. Here's what it bumps: the checkout redesign (estimated +$200K MRR) or the mobile performance work (blocking 3 enterprise deals). Which would you like to deprioritize?" Never just say no — show the cost.

Use data, not opinions

"We interviewed 12 users last month. Zero mentioned this as a top pain point, but 9 mentioned the onboarding confusion. Here's the research." Data doesn't guarantee you win, but it shifts the conversation from opinions to evidence.

Give it a slot, not a bypass

"Great input — I'll add it to the backlog and score it against current items in our next planning cycle." Acknowledges the request, doesn't commit, doesn't dismiss. Most HiPPO items don't survive rigorous scoring.

🚫 The Cost of Doing Nothing

Prioritisation includes the option to do nothing

When evaluating whether to build something, the baseline isn't zero — it's the cost of not building it. Quantify the cost of inaction:

  • How much revenue is being lost per month due to this problem?
  • How many support tickets is this generating? (Support cost per ticket × volume)
  • How many deals are blocked by this missing capability? (ACV × deal count)
  • What is the compounding churn cost if retention stays at current level?

Making the cost of inaction explicit changes prioritisation conversations. "We're losing an estimated $40K/month to this bug" is harder to deprioritize than "users are annoyed by this."

🤝 Stakeholder Negotiation Tactics

SituationTactic
"This is urgent, we need it in 2 weeks"Clarify what "urgent" means: revenue at risk? Legal deadline? Customer commitment? Most urgencies dissolve when you ask what happens if it ships in 6 weeks instead.
Multiple stakeholders want different thingsBring them into the same room. People negotiate differently with each other present than in 1:1s. Shared visibility reduces duplicate pressure.
Stakeholder goes around you to engineeringAddress directly: "I need requests to come through me so I can sequence them against everything else. I can't guarantee quality or timelines if I don't have visibility."
"Competitor just launched this feature"Separate the signal from the urgency. Is this feature actually causing churn? Do users care? Win/loss data is more reliable than feature parity for its own sake.
