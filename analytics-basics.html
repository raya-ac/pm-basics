Analytics Basics

Funnels, cohorts, retention curves — and the gap between data that describes what happened and data that helps you understand why.

Descriptive vs diagnostic analytics

Descriptive analytics answers "what happened": page views were down 12% last week, checkout conversion dropped on mobile, power users retained at 68% in month three. This is table stakes. Every team should be able to answer these questions quickly.

Diagnostic analytics answers "why": which segment drove the drop, which step in the funnel broke, whether the change correlates with a specific release or a seasonality pattern. This is harder, slower, and requires forming hypotheses before you look at data — not after.

The failure mode is treating descriptive analytics as if it were diagnostic. "Revenue is down" is not an insight. "Revenue is down because trial-to-paid conversion dropped for users who signed up via the marketing site but not for those who came through the API" is actionable.

Funnels

A funnel tracks what percentage of users complete each step in a sequence. Signup → onboarding → first core action → paid conversion is a classic SaaS funnel.

The things funnels hide: they show aggregate conversion but not timing, they assume a linear path when user behaviour is often non-linear, and they depend entirely on whether you've instrumented the right events. A funnel built on bad tracking gives confident wrong answers.

When a funnel step drops, the first question is always: did the tracking change, or did user behaviour change? These look identical in the data and require different responses.

Cohort analysis

A cohort is a group of users who share a characteristic at a point in time — most commonly, the week or month they signed up. Cohort analysis tracks how a metric evolves for each cohort over time.

The canonical use case is retention: of users who signed up in January, what percentage were still active in week 2, week 4, week 8? Plotting this as a curve (or a heat map) shows whether retention is improving or degrading across cohorts, which a single aggregate retention number obscures completely.

cohort_month | week_1 | week_2 | week_4 | week_8
-------------+--------+--------+--------+--------
2024-10      |  82%   |  61%   |  44%   |  31%
2024-11      |  84%   |  64%   |  47%   |  34%
2024-12      |  85%   |  66%   |  49%   |  --

The trend here is clear: each cohort retains slightly better. An aggregate retention number would show roughly the same rate each month and miss this signal entirely.

Retention curves

Retention curves show the percentage of a cohort still active at each time interval. A healthy product has a curve that flattens — it drops steeply early (some users churn immediately) then levels off as the retained core stabilises. A curve that keeps declining to zero means you have no long-term users at all.

The flattening point is important. If 30% of users from month one are still active in month six and month twelve, you have a stable retained base. If the curve is still declining at month twelve, you don't — and no amount of new user acquisition will fix that sustainably.

Vanity metrics vs actionable metrics

A vanity metric looks good, goes up, and doesn't tell you whether the product is working. Total registered users, total page views, total app downloads. These numbers grow as long as you keep acquiring users, regardless of whether anyone finds the product useful.

Actionable metrics connect to user behaviour that reflects genuine value: weekly active users who complete the core action, percentage of users who return within 7 days of first use, revenue per retained user. These can go down even when vanity metrics go up, which is why teams resist them.

Simpson's Paradox in product analytics Aggregate metrics can trend in the opposite direction to every individual segment. If your high-retention mobile users grew from 10% to 40% of your user base, overall retention might drop even as both mobile and desktop retention improved individually. Always segment before concluding. If you can't explain why a metric moved at the segment level, you don't understand why it moved.
