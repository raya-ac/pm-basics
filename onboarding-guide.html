Onboarding Engineers

How to ramp up new engineers fast — and set them up to do their best work.

Why Onboarding Matters

A poor onboarding experience costs more than people realise. Engineers who struggle in their first 30 days are significantly more likely to disengage or leave within a year. Studies suggest it takes 8–12 weeks for a new engineer to reach full productivity — bad onboarding can double that.

3x Retention

Engineers with structured onboarding are 3x more likely to stay past year one.

8-Week Ramp

Average time to first meaningful independent contribution with good onboarding.

45-Day Window

First impressions form quickly. Most engineers decide if they'll stay by day 45.

Onboarding Checklist

Dev Environment

Repo cloned, local build works, linter configured, IDE set up, test suite passes.

Access & Permissions

GitHub, Jira/Linear, Slack, staging env, cloud console read access, password manager.

Codebase Orientation

Architecture overview, key services, data models, "how we deploy" doc, runbooks.

Team Process

Sprint cadence, PR review norms, commit message conventions, on-call rotation.

Social

1:1s scheduled with manager and teammates, joined team Slack channels, attended team lunch/coffee.

First Task

A pre-picked "good first issue" — small, well-scoped, with clear acceptance criteria.

Common Onboarding Failures

No clear owner — everyone assumes someone else is running the onboarding. New hire floats for a week.
No starter task — new hire reads docs for two weeks without writing a single line of code.
Firehose of context — six architecture deep-dives in week one. Nothing sticks.
Ignoring culture — technical ramp succeeds but the engineer never understands team norms, values, or how decisions get made.
No feedback loops — no check-ins at 2 weeks, 30 days, or 60 days to catch problems early.

For PMs: How to Help

Share product context early. New engineers do better work when they understand the "why" — the user, the business model, the strategy.
Don't measure velocity in week one. Give new engineers space to explore without feeling evaluated on output.
Introduce them to key stakeholders. A PM intro to design, data, and customer success pays off for months.
Protect their ramp time. Don't pull a new engineer into a critical fire in week two unless absolutely unavoidable.
Ask for their fresh eyes. "What's confusing about this codebase / process?" — new engineers spot problems long-timers are blind to.
