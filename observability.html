Observability

Logs, metrics, and traces -- the three pillars of understanding what your system is doing.

observability monitoring logging metrics sre

The Three Pillars

Observability is the ability to understand the internal state of a system from its external outputs. A system is observable when you can diagnose any failure from the data it emits -- without having to deploy new code to ask new questions.

The three pillars provide complementary views of system behaviour. You need all three to diagnose complex issues in distributed systems:

Pillar 1
Logs
"What happened?"
Timestamped, structured records of discrete events. An HTTP request, an exception thrown, a user action completed. Logs are the most detailed signal -- ideal for debugging specific incidents once you know where to look.
Pillar 2
Metrics
"How much / how fast?"
Numerical measurements aggregated over time: request rate, error count, latency p99, CPU utilisation. Metrics are cheap to store and queryable at any resolution. They power dashboards and alert thresholds.
Pillar 3
Traces
"Where did the time go?"
A trace follows a single request as it travels across multiple services. Each hop is a "span" with its own timing. Traces reveal which service or database call is responsible for slow end-to-end latency.

Key Metrics to Care About

The RED method (Rate, Errors, Duration) and USE method (Utilisation, Saturation, Errors) give teams a practical framework. In practice, focus on these four signals for any user-facing service:

Latency
How long requests take to complete. Always track percentiles, not averages -- p50, p95, and p99 tell very different stories.
p99 < 500ms (typical API)
Error Rate
Percentage of requests returning an error (5xx status codes, exceptions thrown). A sudden spike almost always signals a deployment issue or downstream failure.
< 0.1% for critical paths
Throughput (RPS)
Requests per second processed by the service. Useful for capacity planning and detecting traffic anomalies -- sudden drops can indicate upstream failures.
Baseline + alert on -50%
Saturation
How "full" the service is -- CPU usage, memory pressure, connection pool exhaustion, queue depth. High saturation predicts imminent degradation or failure.
CPU < 70% sustained

Percentile latency matters more than averages. A p99 of 2 seconds means 1 in 100 users experiences a 2-second response -- at 10,000 RPS, that is 100 slow users every second.

SLIs, SLOs, and SLAs Explained

These three terms form the reliability contract between your engineering team, your product, and your customers. They are often confused -- here is a clear breakdown:

SLI
Service Level Indicator
The actual measured metric. A quantitative measure of some aspect of the service. You measure it continuously from real traffic.
"99.3% of API requests in the last 28 days returned a 2xx within 400ms."
SLO
Service Level Objective
The internal target for your SLI. Set by your engineering and product team. Breaching an SLO means you should stop shipping features and fix reliability.
"99.5% of requests should complete successfully within 400ms over any 28-day window."
SLA
Service Level Agreement
The external contract with customers. SLAs typically include financial consequences (credits, refunds) if breached. Should be less strict than your SLO.
"We guarantee 99.9% monthly uptime. Customers receive a 10% credit if we breach this threshold."

The gap between your SLO and your SLA is your error budget -- the amount of unreliability you can "spend" before hitting your customer commitment. Error budgets give teams a data-driven answer to "should we ship this risky change?"

What Makes a Good Alert

Most teams over-alert. Too many alerts leads to alert fatigue, where engineers start ignoring pages because most of them are noise. A good alert should pass all three tests:

Actionable: When this alert fires, the on-call engineer knows exactly what to do. There is a runbook. The alert message includes context, not just a metric name.
Represents user impact: Alert on symptoms (elevated error rate, high p99 latency) rather than causes (CPU at 80%). Causes are useful in dashboards for debugging, not as pages.
Correct severity: Not everything that goes wrong needs to wake someone up at 3am. Use PagerDuty-style severity levels: P1 (customer-impacting, page now), P2 (degraded, notify during business hours), P3 (informational, log and review).
Anti-pattern -- flapping: An alert that fires, resolves, fires, resolves within minutes is misconfigured. It trains engineers to ignore it. Use evaluation windows (e.g. fire only if condition holds for 5 minutes).
Anti-pattern -- too many alerts: If your on-call rotation receives more than 5 pages per shift that require action, your alerting is broken. Treat alert volume as a quality metric -- triage and eliminate noise regularly.

For PMs: Using Observability Data

In incident reviews:

  • Ask to see the metrics dashboard from the incident window -- latency, error rate, RPS. Understanding the shape of the failure helps you ask better "why" questions and write a more accurate customer-facing summary.
  • Ask: "What was our time-to-detect and time-to-resolve?" These are concrete metrics that drive post-mortem action items.
  • If the team "noticed it from a user report" rather than an alert, that is a gap worth fixing -- prioritise adding proper alerting as a follow-up.

In sprint planning:

  • Review error budgets with your SRE or EM before each planning cycle. If you are close to burning your SLO, reliability work should displace feature work.
  • Ask for p95 and p99 latency for your core user flows. If checkout or search are slow at the 95th percentile, that is a product quality issue -- not just a backend concern.
  • Treat "add observability" as a launch requirement for new features, not an afterthought. If you can't measure a feature's impact on system health, you're flying blind after you ship it.
  • Dashboard reviews in retros are a healthy habit. Having the team look at a week of production metrics together builds shared intuition about what "normal" looks like.
