How search engines work and what actually moves the needle on organic traffic.

How Search Works: Crawl, Index, Rank

Before your page can rank, Google (and other search engines) need to discover it, understand it, and decide where it belongs in results. This happens in three stages:

Stage 1
Crawl
Googlebot discovers pages by following links across the web. It downloads the HTML of each page it finds and queues new links for future crawling. If a page has no inbound links, it may never be crawled.
→
Stage 2
Index
Crawled pages are processed and stored in Google's index -- a giant catalogue of content keyed to the words and concepts on each page. Pages blocked by robots.txt, noindex tags, or slow load times may not get indexed.
→
Stage 3
Rank
When someone searches, Google scores every indexed page for that query across hundreds of signals -- relevance, authority, page experience -- and returns the top results. Ranking is dynamic and personalised.

The practical implication: you can have brilliant content that never ranks because it can't be crawled, isn't indexed, or lacks the authority signals to compete. Technical SEO fixes the first two; content and links fix the third.

On-Page SEO

On-page SEO is everything you control on the page itself. It signals to search engines what the page is about and who should see it:

Title Tag
The most important on-page signal. Appears in the browser tab and as the blue headline in search results. Should include the primary keyword near the front. Keep under 60 characters.
<title>Docker Basics for PMs -- ref.guide</title>
Meta Description
A summary snippet shown under the title in results. Does not directly affect ranking, but a compelling description improves click-through rate. Keep under 160 characters.
<meta name="description" content="...">
Heading Hierarchy
One H1 per page (matches the topic), logical H2s for sections, H3s for sub-sections. Search engines use headings to understand structure. Users scan them to navigate -- both matter.
H1 > H2 > H3 (never skip levels)
URL Structure
Clean, descriptive, keyword-rich URLs. Use hyphens between words, not underscores. Keep them short. Avoid query strings and dynamic parameters in crawlable content URLs.
/seo/on-page-basics (good) /page?id=2847 (bad)
Image Alt Text
Describes images to search engines (which can't see them) and screen readers. Include the keyword naturally where relevant. Don't keyword-stuff alt text -- describe the image accurately.
alt="Docker container architecture diagram"
Internal Linking
Links between your own pages pass authority and help crawlers discover content. Use descriptive anchor text that reflects the target page's topic rather than generic "click here" phrases.
"see our guide to Kubernetes" (not "click here")

Technical SEO

Technical SEO ensures search engines can efficiently crawl, index, and understand your site. Many technical issues silently tank rankings without any obvious user-facing symptom:

Off-Page SEO

Off-page SEO covers signals that originate outside your website -- primarily the quality and quantity of external sites that link to you. Google interprets links as votes of authority.

  • Backlinks: Links from other domains to your pages. A link from a high-authority site (major news outlet, well-known industry blog) carries far more weight than dozens of low-quality links. Quality beats quantity.
  • Domain Authority: A third-party metric (Moz, Ahrefs, Semrush each have their own version) that estimates how strong your overall domain is based on its backlink profile. Not a Google metric, but a useful proxy.
  • Brand Signals: Unlinked brand mentions, social shares, and branded searches all send indirect signals. A strong brand that people actively search for is harder for Google to ignore.
  • Link building strategies: Creating genuinely useful content that earns links ("link-worthy" content), digital PR, guest posts on relevant publications, partnerships, and fixing broken links on other sites that once pointed to you.

Avoid low-quality link schemes (paid links, link farms, private blog networks). Google's spam systems detect and penalise these. One strong editorial link is worth more than hundreds of spammy ones.

Core Web Vitals

Core Web Vitals are Google's specific set of page experience metrics that are used as a ranking signal. They measure real-world user experience -- load speed, interactivity, and visual stability:

LCP
Largest Contentful Paint
Measures loading performance. The time until the largest visible content element (image or text block) is rendered on screen.
Good: < 2.5s   Needs work: 2.5-4s   Poor: > 4s
INP
Interaction to Next Paint
Measures interactivity (replaced FID in 2024). The latency from any user interaction (click, tap, keypress) to the next frame painted on screen.
Good: < 200ms   Needs work: 200-500ms   Poor: > 500ms
CLS
Cumulative Layout Shift
Measures visual stability. A score of unexpected layout shifts -- ads loading late, images without dimensions, fonts swapping. A high CLS means the page jumps around as it loads.
Good: < 0.1   Needs work: 0.1-0.25   Poor: > 0.25

Measure your CWVs in Google Search Console (real user data) and PageSpeed Insights (lab data). Field data from real users is what Google uses for ranking -- lab scores are useful for debugging.

SEO vs SEM: Organic vs Paid

SEO -- Organic Search
  • No cost per click
  • Compounding returns over time
  • Takes months to see results
  • Hard to turn on and off
  • High trust from users
  • Best for: content, informational queries, long-term growth
SEM -- Paid Search (Google Ads)
  • Pay per click (CPC bidding)
  • Instant traffic from day one
  • Stops immediately when budget stops
  • Full control over timing and targeting
  • Labelled "Sponsored" -- lower trust
  • Best for: launches, commercial queries, testing messaging

The two are complementary, not competitive. Use paid search to validate that a keyword converts before investing months of SEO effort into it. Use SEO to reduce long-term customer acquisition cost for queries you've validated.

For PMs: SEO Priorities and Dev Conflicts

How to prioritise SEO work:

  • Focus first on technical foundations -- if pages can't be crawled and indexed, no amount of content helps. Run a site audit (Screaming Frog, Semrush) and fix crawl errors, noindex tags, and redirect chains before investing in content.
  • Prioritise high-traffic, high-intent pages first. A 20% improvement in organic traffic to your top 10 pages will dwarf a 100% improvement to your bottom 200.
  • Core Web Vitals improvements have dual value: they improve search rankings and conversion rates simultaneously. Make the business case on both dimensions.
  • Track SEO as a channel metric in your product reviews: organic sessions, keyword rankings for target terms, click-through rates from Search Console.

Common dev/SEO conflicts to navigate:

Single-Page Apps (SPAs) and client-side rendering
React, Vue, and Angular SPAs that render content in the browser (client-side) can be invisible to search engines. Googlebot does execute JavaScript, but it can be slow and unreliable. Push for server-side rendering (SSR) or static generation (SSG) for SEO-critical pages. Next.js and Nuxt are common solutions.
Pagination and infinite scroll
Infinite scroll is great UX but terrible for SEO -- the content below the fold never gets indexed because Googlebot doesn't scroll. Use paginated URLs (/products?page=2) for category pages, or implement "load more" with a crawlable fallback pagination. Blog and product listing pages are especially affected.
Redirects and URL changes
Changing a URL without a 301 redirect is link equity suicide -- all the authority built up at the old URL is lost. Any URL change (restructuring the nav, rebranding a product line) needs a permanent redirect from old to new. Temporary 302 redirects do not pass authority. Always audit redirects before major site migrations.
