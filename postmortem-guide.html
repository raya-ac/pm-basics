Postmortem Guide

How to run blameless postmortems that actually improve your system — and your team's trust.

⚙ Why Blameless Postmortems

Core principle: Every failure is a failure of the system, not an individual. People act rationally given the information and tools available to them at the time. Blame silences the truth; blameless postmortems surface it.

What you gain

  • Honest timelines — people report what actually happened
  • Root causes, not surface symptoms
  • Psychological safety to speak up next incident
  • Institutional memory that outlasts any single engineer
  • Measurable improvement over time via action items

What blame costs you

  • Engineers hide near-misses to avoid punishment
  • Root causes stay buried — problems recur
  • Top performers leave when blamed publicly
  • Incidents take longer to resolve (people hesitant to act)
  • Culture of fear replaces culture of learning

⚠ Severity Levels

SeverityDefinitionExamplesResponsePostmortem Required?

🕑 Writing Timeline

During incident
Live incident document
Keep a running log in a shared doc or incident channel. Timestamp every significant action. Capture hypotheses tested, commands run, alerts fired. This is raw material — accuracy over prose.
Within 24 hours
Draft timeline + initial facts
While memory is fresh, produce a factual timeline. Tag who did what. Identify the contributing factors you already know. No conclusions yet — just facts.
Within 48–72 hours
Postmortem meeting
30–60 min meeting with all responders. Walk the timeline. Apply 5-Whys or fishbone to root causes. Agree on action items with owners and due dates. A facilitator who was NOT the on-call responder runs the meeting.
Within 1 week
Published postmortem
Finalize and share broadly. Send to eng-all, leadership summary optional. The doc lives in your postmortem index permanently.
Within 30 days
Action item follow-up
Review completion of all action items in the incident retrospective or sprint review. Blocked items escalate. Don't let action items rot — it signals postmortems don't matter.

📄 The 7-Section Structure

1. Summary

2–4 sentences. What broke, when, how many users affected, how it was resolved. Written for a non-technical executive. Example: "On 2024-03-15, the checkout service experienced a 47-minute outage affecting ~12,000 users due to an uncaught nil pointer after a config deploy. No data was lost."

2. Impact

Quantify everything: duration, % users affected, revenue impact, error rates, SLO burn. Use data from your observability tools. Stakeholders need numbers, not vague descriptions like "many users."

3. Timeline

Chronological log with UTC timestamps. Include: first alert, when responders joined, hypotheses tested, mitigation applied, full resolution. Keep it factual — no editorializing.

4. Root Cause Analysis

The real why, not the proximate trigger. Use 5-Whys or fishbone. A deploy caused a crash, but WHY was there no test? WHY did the test gap exist? Work until you hit a systemic factor.

5. Contributing Factors

List every factor that made the incident worse or harder to detect: missing alerts, confusing runbooks, unclear ownership, lack of feature flags, insufficient load testing.

6. What Went Well

Essential and often skipped. What did the team do right? Fast detection? Good communication? Effective runbook? Reinforce these behaviors explicitly.

7. Action Items

Every action item needs: a clear description, an owner (one person), a due date, and a ticket link. Categorize by type: Prevent Detect Mitigate Process

❓ 5-Whys Example

Incident: Production database ran out of disk space, causing 2-hour downtime.
Why #1
The database ran out of disk space
→ Because the disk filled up with transaction logs
Why #2
Transaction logs were not being rotated
→ Because the log rotation cron job was disabled
Why #3
The cron job was disabled
→ Because an engineer disabled it during debugging 3 weeks ago and forgot to re-enable it
Why #4
No alert existed for the disabled cron
→ Because cron jobs are not monitored for execution frequency
Why #5 — Root Cause
No standard for monitoring scheduled jobs
→ Action item: Add heartbeat monitoring to all production cron jobs. Owner: platform team. Due: 2024-04-01.

💬 Blameless Language Guide

Avoid (blame-ful)Use instead (blameless)Why it matters
"Alice forgot to test the change""The change was deployed without a test covering this code path"Points to the system gap, not the person
"Bob made a mistake""The configuration value was set incorrectly"Passive voice removes personal blame
"The engineer should have known""The documentation did not make this behavior clear"Focuses on missing information, not competence
"Careless deployment""Deployment proceeded without a canary phase"Identifies the missing safeguard
"Obvious error""The failure mode was not surfaced by existing tooling""Obvious" in hindsight is never obvious in the moment
"Negligence""The process did not include a verification step"Blame never improves a process

✅ Good vs Bad Action Items

Bad action itemGood action itemWhat changed
"Be more careful with deploys""Add automated rollback trigger when error rate exceeds 1% within 5 min of deploy — owner: @carlos, due: 2024-04-15, ticket: ENG-1204"Specific, measurable, owned, time-bound
"Fix monitoring""Create alert for disk utilization >80% on prod DB nodes — owner: @infra-team, due: 2024-03-22"Concrete threshold, clear owner
"Improve communication""Add #incident-comms channel to runbook as mandatory update destination for SEV-1/2 — owner: @eng-ops, due: 2024-03-25"Actionable change to a specific artifact
"Train the team""Schedule 60-min incident response drill for all on-call engineers by end of Q2 — owner: @eng-manager"Specific activity with deadline
Rule of thumb: If an action item cannot be verified as "done" or "not done" in 30 days, rewrite it. Vague AIs are abandoned AIs.
